# Discovery notes — community clinic check-in (design-thinking input)

**Design challenge owner:** A small nonprofit community health clinic.

**Who:** Walk-in patients at a free community clinic. The primary user we
observed is Marisol, 58, who speaks limited English, takes the bus, and brings
her teenage grandson to help translate.

**What we observed (raw notes from 6 shadowing sessions + 4 interviews):**

- Patients arrive and stand in a line that isn't clearly a line; several people
  weren't sure if they were "checked in" or just waiting.
- The paper intake form is in English only. Marisol and three others handed it to
  a companion or a front-desk volunteer to fill out, visibly embarrassed.
- The clipboard form asks for an address; two unhoused patients left it blank and
  later worried it would disqualify them.
- Average wait was 70+ minutes; there's no indication of where you are in line,
  so people kept walking up to the desk to ask, which interrupted the one
  volunteer doing check-in.
- Several patients said they almost didn't come because last time they "waited
  for hours and then got turned away" for a missing document.
- One quote (Marisol, via grandson): "I don't mind waiting. I mind not knowing if
  I did it right, or if they forgot me."
- A volunteer said: "We lose people who give up and leave before we ever see
  them."

**Constraints noted:** the clinic has one front-desk volunteer at a time, no
budget for new hardware beyond a donated tablet or two, multilingual patient
base (Spanish, Vietnamese, English), and many patients have low digital literacy.

**What success would look like (owner's words):** fewer people giving up and
leaving, and patients feeling sure they're "in the system" and will be seen.
