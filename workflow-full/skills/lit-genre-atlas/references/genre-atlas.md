**Compiled:** 2026-07-06. **Method:** WebSearch evidence only (WebFetch not used — proxy risk;
all claims below are from search-result snippets plus compiler domain knowledge where marked

percentages as directional, not audited.

**Field template per genre:** Reader promise / conventions · Word-count band (debut) ·

## 1. FANTASY — Epic / High

**Reader promise:** A fully-built secondary world with consistent magic, large-scale stakes
(kingdom/world), a long journey/quest arc, and payoff of long-seeded prophecy/politics.
Readers expect immersion and scope; multi-book commitment is the norm. [K + sources]

**Word count (debut):** 90,000–120,000; agents tolerate >100k for SFF specifically; stay
under ~120k as a debut (established authors run 150k+). (Manuscript Academy / Reedsy)

**Successful works:**
- *The Lord of the Rings* — J.R.R. Tolkien (1954). Canonical template: quest, Dark Lord, fellowship; the genre's shared reference point. [K]
- *A Game of Thrones* — George R.R. Martin (1996). Modern standard-bearer for grimdark political epic; proved adult epic could dominate mainstream culture. [K]
- *The Way of Kings* (Stormlight Archive) — Brandon Sanderson (2010). Standard-bearer for hard magic systems; Sanderson's 2022 Kickstarter ($41M) proved direct-to-reader epic fantasy demand. [K]
- *The Will of the Many* — James Islington (2023). Recent breakout blending epic fantasy with academy/progression beats; BookTok-boosted. [K]
- *The Sword of Kaigen* — M.L. Wang (2019). Self-pub success (SPFBO winner) showing standalone military epic can break out outside tradpub. [K]

**Signature tropes:** chosen one; the quest; Dark Lord; wise mentor; found fellowship;
prophecy; medieval-European-derived settings (increasingly subverted — female/queer/POC
protagonists, non-Western mythologies). (Reedsy, MasterClass, fictionphile)

**Market signal:** Stable-to-growing inside a booming SFF category (SFF market value +41.3%
in 2024, largely romantasy-driven). Sells tradpub hardback/series + strong KU/audio
(Audible) tail. (Medium/BookTok piece; Bloomberg)

**Sources:**

- https://www.masterclass.com/articles/how-to-use-fantasy-tropes-effectively

## 2. FANTASY — Urban

**Reader promise:** Fantasy conventions collided with a modern (usually city) setting;
either a Masquerade (hidden supernatural society) or open alternate history; fast, gritty,
often crime-shaped plots with noir vibes; series with an episodic case-per-book engine.
(TV Tropes; British Fantasy Society)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000. [K, consistent with general adult-fantasy guidance]

**Successful works:**
- *Neverwhere* — Neil Gaiman (1996). Literary-leaning classic of the hidden-city mode. [K]
- *Storm Front* (Dresden Files) — Jim Butcher (2000). Standard-bearer; "set a lot of urban fantasy tropes in stone" — wizard-for-hire, supernatural crime-solving; spawned hundreds of imitators; GraphicAudio adaptations from 2025. (TV Tropes / Wikipedia / BookRiot via search)
- *Rivers of London* — Ben Aaronovitch (2011). Modern police-procedural urban fantasy standard. [K]
- *The Atlas Six* — Olivie Blake (2020). Recent breakout: self-published, went viral on BookTok, acquired by Tor — the model self-pub→tradpub crossover in contemporary fantasy. [K]

**Signature tropes:** the Masquerade; wizard PI / magical law enforcement; vampires running
nightclubs; ancient beings in modern jobs; snarky first-person narrator; escalating
city-threat per book. (TV Tropes; mdmassey.com)

**Market signal:** Mature/stable — its 2000s–2010s boom cooled; now steady in KU
self-pub and long-running tradpub series; energy migrated to romantasy and cozy fantasy. [K]

## 3. FANTASY — Romantasy

**Reader promise:** Full fantasy worldbuilding AND a central romance that must pay off
(HEA/HFN by series end); high heat is expected in the adult lane; the romance arc is
load-bearing, not a subplot. Failure to deliver the couple is a contract breach. [K + HEA sources]

**Word count (debut):** ~100,000–120,000 (fantasy-length; bestsellers run longer). [K]

**Successful works:**
- *Outlander* — Diana Gabaldon (1991). Canonical antecedent of romance-fantasy crossover at scale. [K]
- *A Court of Thorns and Roses* — Sarah J. Maas (2015). The genre's foundation stone; Maas sales +161% in Bloomsbury FY2023-24, driving a record 30% publisher revenue jump. (Bloomberg/KPBS via search)
- *Fourth Wing* — Rebecca Yarros (2023). Recent breakout that industrialized the genre; sequel *Onyx Storm* sold 1M+ copies in week one (Q1 2025) — record-breaking. (Bloomberg via search)
- *From Blood and Ash* — Jennifer L. Armentrout (2020). Self-pub romantasy phenomenon proving the indie lane. [K]
- *Powerless* — Lauren Roberts (2023). Self-pub (B&N Press/KDP) → Simon & Schuster; 19 weeks #1 NYT YA; 1M copies in 9 months; TV deal. (Wikipedia/Deadline via search)

**Signature tropes:** enemies-to-lovers; fated mates; fae courts; dragon riders; magic
school/war college; "touch her and die"; one-bed; morally gray love interest. [K + Bloomberg]

**Market signal:** The hottest genre in publishing: $610M projected 2024 (from $454M 2023);
11M units in first 5 months of 2024 (~2x YoY); 1 in 4 NYT hardcover fiction bestsellers in
2024; Circana warned of saturation from 2025. Sells everywhere: tradpub lead titles, KU
self-pub, BookTok-driven (691k "romantasy" mentions in 2024). (Bloomberg, fanbolt, KPBS)

## 4. FANTASY — Progression / LitRPG

**Reader promise:** Measurable, frequent power advancement (levels, stats, cultivation
stages) — the progression IS the plot engine; game-mechanic transparency (stat sheets/blue
boxes in LitRPG); long series; consistent update cadence when serialized. Stagnation or
off-screen power-ups break the contract. [K + Royal Road sources]

**Word count:** Serial-first: chapters ~1,500–2,500 words (phone reading), 20k-word launch
dump then daily chapters for a month; "book" compilations for KU commonly 150,000–250,000
words — the one genre where longer sells better. (Royal Road forums / Chapter Chronicles;
[K] for compilation lengths)

**Successful works:**
- *Mother of Learning* — Domagoj Kurmaić (web serial 2011–2020). Canonical free-serial time-loop progression classic. [K]
- *Unsouled* (Cradle) — Will Wight (2016). Standard-bearer: NYT and #1 Kindle bestselling self-pub cultivation series; all-in on KU + Travis Baldree audio. (Amazon listings via search)
- *Dungeon Crawler Carl* — Matt Dinniman (Royal Road 2020 → self-pub → Ace/PRH print 2024, author retained ebook rights). 10M+ copies across 8 books by Dec 2025; mainstreamed LitRPG (Slate praise, 2026). (Wikipedia via search)
- *He Who Fights with Monsters* — Shirtaloon (Royal Road → KU). Perennial top KU earner; the template RR→Amazon pipeline. [K + search confirmation it's marketed alongside Cradle]
- *Beware of Chicken* — CasualFarmer (Royal Road → Podium). Cozy-progression hybrid hit showing subgenre cross-pollination. [K]

**Signature tropes:** system apocalypse; dungeon crawls; stat screens; cultivation
realms/sects; weak-to-strong underdog; isekai/portal openings; crafting/kingdom-building;
snarky AI or system voice. [K + Royal Road forum threads]

**Market signal:** Fast-growing; the flagship serial-to-market genre. Sells via Royal
Road (free serial) → Patreon (median 6 advance chapters; ~13% follower→patron conversion;
~$650/mo median at 1k followers) → KU "stub-and-publish" → audiobook (often the biggest
revenue). Tradpub now acquiring print rights (DCC/Ace). (Chapter Chronicles; Jane Friedman)

**Sources:**
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungeon_Crawler_Carl
- https://www.chapterchronicles.com/blog/royal-road-patreon-2025/
- https://janefriedman.com/the-serial-first-model-how-royal-road-powers-direct-to-fan-author-careers/
- https://www.royalroad.com/forums/thread/146822

## 5. FANTASY — Cozy

**Reader promise:** Low stakes, comfort, and a guaranteed happy/warm ending in a secondary
world; slice-of-life over epic quests; no beloved character dies traumatically; humor and
community. "Cozy" ≠ no conflict — but the reader is promised safety. (cozyescapism;
Ingram; winteriscoming)

**Word count (debut):** 70,000–90,000; novellas common and accepted. [K]

**Successful works:**
- *Howl's Moving Castle* — Diana Wynne Jones (1986). Canonical gentle-fantasy antecedent. [K]
- *The House in the Cerulean Sea* — TJ Klune (2020). Tradpub standard-bearer for found-family warmth; long-run bestseller. [K]
- *Legends & Lattes* — Travis Baldree (2022). Recent breakout AND self-pub success: "high fantasy, low stakes" trailblazer, self-published then acquired by Tor; credited with launching the subgenre wave. (Goodreads/Medium via search)
- *A Wizard's Guide to Defensive Baking* — T. Kingfisher (2020). Self-pub Hugo winner adjacent to the cozy register. [K]
- *Beware of Chicken* — CasualFarmer. Serial-side cozy-progression crossover hit. [K]

**Signature tropes:** retired adventurer opens a shop; coffee/tea/baking; found family;
low-stakes entrepreneurship; queer-normative worlds; small magical communities; happy
ending required. (cozyescapism; Vice; mygeekology)

**Market signal:** Growing — a post-2022 boom ("powerful hold on publishing right now,"
comfort-reading demand); sells tradpub trade paperback + self-pub/KU + strong audio.
(Ingram; winteriscoming)

**Sources:**
- https://cozyescapism.com/what-makes-cozy-fantasy-cozy/
- https://www.ingramcontent.com/libraries-blog/cozy-science-fiction-and-fantasy-is-the-subgenre-the-world-and-your-library-needs-right-now
- https://winteriscoming.net/why-cozy-fantasy-is-taking-over-the-genre-and-5-books-to-try
- https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/61242426-legends-lattes

## 6. SCIENCE FICTION — Hard SF

**Reader promise:** Scientific plausibility is the contract — technology and physics must
be consistent with (or rigorously extrapolated from) current understanding; problem-solving
via real science is the pleasure; an author caught hand-waving loses the reader. (space.com;
movieweb)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–115,000; technical exposition can justify the high end.
(WordTally/Manuscript Academy)

**Successful works:**
- *Rendezvous with Rama* — Arthur C. Clarke (1973). Canonical big-object/pure-wonder hard SF. [K]
- *The Martian* — Andy Weir (2011 self-pub → Crown 2014). Standard-bearer AND self-pub legend: proved hard-science problem-solving could be a mainstream page-turner. (slashfilm/movieweb via search)
- *The Three-Body Problem* — Liu Cixin (2008/tr. 2014). Hugo winner; globalized hard SF and drove a translation wave. [K]
- *Project Hail Mary* — Andy Weir (2021). Recent breakout walking the hard/speculative line; major 2026 film adaptation. (space.com via search)

**Signature tropes:** competence porn; lone engineer vs. environment; first contact via
physics/linguistics; generation ships; terraforming; "science the hell out of it";
show-your-work exposition. [K + search]

**Market signal:** Stable niche inside growing SFF; tradpub-led, strong audio;
self-pub viable (Weir's origin). [K]

**Sources:**
- https://www.space.com/entertainment/space-movies-shows/how-the-project-hail-mary-book-walks-the-line-between-hard-and-speculative-science-fiction-and-why-the-film-did-not
- https://movieweb.com/best-hard-sci-fi-books-21st-century/
- https://wordtally.net/guides/novel-word-count-by-genre

## 7. SCIENCE FICTION — Space Opera

**Reader promise:** Scale and adventure — interstellar settings, empires/politics, war,
big casts, melodramatic sweep; scientific rigor is optional but internal consistency is
not; series-length payoff of galactic-stakes arcs. [K + Expanse coverage]

**Word count (debut):** 90,000–120,000. [K, per SFF norms above]

**Successful works:**
- *Dune* — Frank Herbert (1965). Canonical planetary-empire epic; still a first-contact point for the genre. [K]
- *Leviathan Wakes* (The Expanse) — James S.A. Corey (2011). Standard-bearer: "as hard as a space opera can get," politics-forward solar-system realism; TV adaptation. (movieweb via search)
- *Ancillary Justice* — Ann Leckie (2013). Swept Hugo/Nebula/Clarke; reset expectations for POV and empire critique. [K]
- *A Memory Called Empire* — Arkady Martine (2019). Recent breakout: 2020 Hugo Best Novel debut; sequel *A Desolation Called Peace* won the 2022 Hugo — the most-celebrated 2020s space-opera franchise. (Wikipedia/metastellar via search)
- *We Are Legion (We Are Bob)* (Bobiverse) — Dennis E. Taylor (2016). Self-pub/Audible-first success showing indie space opera's audio-led lane. [K]

**Signature tropes:** galactic empire; ragtag crew on a ship; AI companions/ship minds;
ancient precursor tech; space battles; court intrigue; the station/belt underclass. [K]

**Market signal:** Stable; tradpub series + very strong audiobook economics; indie lane
is audio-led (Audible originals). [K]

**Sources:**
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A_Memory_Called_Empire
- https://www.metastellar.com/nonfiction/news/a-memory-called-empire-wins-the-2020-hugo-award-for-best-novel/
- https://phillipstrang.com/best-space-opera-novels-2/

## 8. SCIENCE FICTION — Dystopian

**Reader promise:** A systematically oppressive society whose mechanics mirror/diagnose
real anxieties (surveillance, authoritarianism, environmental collapse, class); the
protagonist's awakening and resistance; the warning is the point — endings may be bleak
(adult) or hopeful-through-revolt (YA). (EBSCO; Medium via search)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000 adult; 60,000–85,000 YA. [K + YA guidance]

**Successful works:**
- *1984* — George Orwell (1949). Canonical; 30M+ copies sold; the genre's vocabulary source. (search evidence)
- *The Handmaid's Tale* — Margaret Atwood (1985). Canonical feminist dystopia; TV-era resurgence. (search evidence)
- *The Hunger Games* — Suzanne Collins (2008). Standard-bearer; 100M+ copies; defined YA dystopia's 2010s boom. (search evidence)
- *Sunrise on the Reaping* — Suzanne Collins (2025). Recent breakout: 1.8M+ copies since March 2025 release amid a 2025 dystopia resurgence. (Publishers Weekly/screenrant via search)
- *Wool* — Hugh Howey (2011). Self-pub serial (KDP shorts) → phenomenon → Silo TV series; the classic self-pub dystopia case. [K]

**Signature tropes:** oppressive regime + surveillance; rebellion arc; sorting/castes;
forbidden knowledge; the spectacle (games, ritual); unreliable official history. [K]

**Market signal:** Resurgent — screenrant reports a 2025 resurgence; one search source
claims a 37% sales surge as readers use dystopia as a "diagnostic tool" (single-source,
directional). Sells tradpub + huge backlist; YA lane cyclical. (screenrant; Medium)

**Sources:**
- https://screenrant.com/dystopian-books-genre-resurgence-2025-hunger-games-watch-me-explainer/
- https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/utopian-and-dystopian-fiction
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Handmaid%27s_Tale

## 9. SCIENCE FICTION — Climate (Cli-Fi)

**Reader promise:** Climate change and its societal impact is the story's engine, not
backdrop; grounded extrapolation (often research-forward); ranges dystopian-to-hopeful
("hopepunk"/solutions strand growing); overlaps literary fiction heavily. (Wikipedia; Grist)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000 (literary norms apply when it's litfic-coded). [K]

**Successful works:**
- *Parable of the Sower* — Octavia Butler (1993). Canonical; posthumously hit the NYT list in 2020 as climate anxiety mainstreamed. [K]
- *The Overstory* — Richard Powers (2018). Pulitzer-winning literary standard-bearer for ecological fiction. [K]
- *The Ministry for the Future* — Kim Stanley Robinson (2020). Genre-cementing: presidential/UN mentions, Pentagon invitation; the policy-world crossover book. (Wikipedia via search)
- *The Light Pirate* — Lily Brooks-Dalton (2022). Recent breakout (GMA Book Club); climate-collapse Florida as mainstream book-club fiction. [K]

**Signature tropes:** the flooded/burning near-future city; climate refugees; geoengineering
gambits; intergenerational guilt; institutional protagonists (ministries, NGOs); hope as
a deliberate act. [K + Grist]

**Market signal:** Growing — "rapidly growing genre" as literary and SF authors converge
on it; primarily tradpub/literary imprints and book clubs, not KU. (Wikipedia; NYPL)

**Sources:**

## 10. ROMANCE — Contemporary (+ the HEA/HFN convention)

**Reader promise — THE genre contract:** A central love story with an emotionally
satisfying, optimistic ending (RWA definition). **HEA** (Happily Ever After: lasting,
permanent couple happiness) or at minimum **HFN** (Happy For Now: together and happy,
future open — accepted mid-series; HEA closes the series). "If it doesn't have an HEA (or
HFN), it's not romance" — a couple that doesn't end up together is a mis-shelved tragedy
and readers treat it as betrayal. Both leads' POVs/arcs matter; the relationship is the
A-plot. (BookRiot; writingmastery; RWA via search)

**Word count (debut):** 70,000–90,000 single-title; category romance (Harlequin) 50,000–70,000;
romantic suspense 80,000–100,000. (Manuscript Academy / WordTally)

**Successful works:**
- *Pride and Prejudice* — Jane Austen (1813). The canonical enemies-to-lovers source code. [K]
- *The Hating Game* — Sally Thorne (2016). Modern rom-com standard-bearer that re-ignited the trade-paperback rom-com. [K]
- *It Ends with Us* — Colleen Hoover (2016). BookTok-era breakout: originally indie-adjacent author whose backlist resurged to dominate bestseller lists 2021–24; film 2024. (accio/BookTok analyses via search)
- *Beach Read* / *Great Big Beautiful Life* — Emily Henry (2020/2025). The current tradpub standard-bearer; every release an instant #1. (accio via search)
- *The Love Hypothesis* — Ali Hazelwood (2021). Breakout with self-pub DNA (originated as Star Wars fanfic); launched the STEM-romance wave. (accio via search)

**Signature tropes:** enemies-to-lovers; fake dating; second-chance; forced proximity/one
bed; grumpy-sunshine; small-town return; workplace rivals. (accio; ResearchGate BookTok study)

**Market signal:** Romance is publishing's highest-earning fiction genre (~$1.44B revenue;
$1.08B 2023 retail est.); romance = ~55% of KU borrows (2023); self-pub ≈ 70% of genre
sales — the most indie-dominated major genre; BookTok is the discovery engine. (WordsRated;
gitnux; authoreverafter — figures directional)

**Sources:**
- https://bookriot.com/if-it-doesnt-have-an-hea-or-hfn-its-not-romance/
- https://www.writingmastery.com/blog/hea-vs-hfn-understanding-happy-endings-in-romance-fiction
- https://wordsrated.com/romance-novel-sales-statistics/
- https://www.accio.com/business/contemporary-romance-bestsellers

## 11. ROMANCE — Historical

**Reader promise:** HEA/HFN, period-authentic texture (Regency dominant) with modern
emotional sensibility; society's constraints (rank, reputation, marriage market) generate
the conflict; readers accept anachronistic agency but not a broken HEA. [K + TV Tropes]

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000. [K]

**Successful works:**
- *Regency Buck* / Georgette Heyer's oeuvre (1930s–50s). Canonical: Heyer invented the Regency romance. [K]
- *Devil in Winter* — Lisa Kleypas (2006). Standard-bearer of the modern historical craft. [K]
- *The Duke and I* (Bridgertons) — Julia Quinn (2000). Netflix-era breakout: the 2020 adaptation sent the whole subgenre's backlist onto bestseller lists; each book showcases a different trope (fake courtship, forbidden love...). (TV Tropes/Substack via search)
- *Bringing Down the Duke* — Evie Dunmore (2019). Recent breakout lane: suffragist historical romance. [K]

**Signature tropes:** fake courtship; marriage of convenience; rake reformed; wallflower;
scandal/compromise; the Season and the ton; secret identity (Lady Whistledown-style). (TV Tropes; nataliejane.substack)

**Market signal:** Stable with Netflix-driven spikes; tradpub mass-market + deep KU
self-pub Regency ecosystem (Amazon maintains dedicated KU Regency storefronts). (Amazon via search)

**Sources:**

## 12. ROMANCE — Paranormal

**Reader promise:** HEA/HFN with supernatural leads in a recognizable (usually modern)
world; romance is the A-plot, the paranormal world the B-plot; one lead "more human" as
reader anchor; danger/forbidden-ness of the supernatural lover is the draw; consistent
magic/species rules. (clairestaleyediting; rivereditor via search)

**Word count (debut):** 70,000–90,000. [K]

**Successful works:**
- *Twilight* — Stephenie Meyer (2005). Canonical phenomenon (~160M copies [K]) that defined the vampire-romance contract for a generation.
- *Dark Lover* (Black Dagger Brotherhood) — J.R. Ward (2005). Long-series standard-bearer of adult PNR. [K]
- *Kate Daniels* series — Ilona Andrews (2007–). UF/PNR boundary standard with devoted fandom. [K]
- *The Serpent and the Wings of Night* — Carissa Broadbent (2022). Recent breakout: self-published vampire romantasy hit, acquired by Tor/Bramble — the PNR→romantasy revival in action. [K]

**Signature tropes:** fated mates ("insta-love but make it supernatural"); vampire
seduction/blood intimacy; shifter packs and scent-recognition; forbidden human-supernatural
love; immortal age gaps; mating bonds. (clairestaleyediting; underthecoversbookblog)

**Market signal:** Revived — the 2000s PNR boom cooled, then re-emerged rebranded through
romantasy (vampire/fae romance); heavy KU self-pub presence (shifter romance is a KU
staple); tradpub re-entering via romantasy imprints (Bramble). [K + sources]

**Sources:**

- https://www.underthecoversbookblog.com/paranormal-romance-genre-guide/

## 13. MYSTERY/CRIME — Cozy Mystery

**Reader promise:** Murder without trauma: NO graphic violence, on-page sex, or swearing;
no gruesome forensics or serial-killer POV; an amateur sleuth (baker, librarian,
shop-owner) in a small/quirky community; puzzle + charm; order restored by the end —
justice always lands. Series form is expected (publishers buy in threes). (cozycrimereads;
PBS Masterpiece; authors.ai)

**Word count (debut):** 60,000–80,000 — deliberately shorter and lighter. (WordTally/Manuscript Academy)

**Successful works:**
- *The Murder at the Vicarage* (Miss Marple) — Agatha Christie (1930). Canonical amateur-sleuth village template. [K]
- *Still Life* (Three Pines) — Louise Penny (2005). Literary-leaning modern standard-bearer. [K]
- *The Thursday Murder Club* — Richard Osman (2020). Recent breakout: 45k copies in 3 days, Sunday Times #1, millions sold worldwide, film adaptation — pulled cozy crime to the mainstream. (search evidence)
- *Vera Wong's Unsolicited Advice for Murderers* — Jesse Q. Sutanto (2023). Breakout Edgar (Best Original Paperback) + Audie winner; the model contemporary cozy. (search evidence)
- KU cozy series ecosystems (culinary/craft/pet cozies) — the genre's large self-pub lane. [K]

**Signature tropes:** amateur sleuth with a day job (bakery/bookstore/tea shop); small-town
gossip network; animal sidekick; recipes/crafts as texture; bumbling-or-friendly local
police; red herrings; the community dinner-table denouement. (cozycrimereads; Killer Nashville)

**Market signal:** Growing — "one of the most beloved and commercially successful
subgenres," with an Osman-led cosy-crime revival in tradpub and an evergreen KU series
economy. (rivereditor; search evidence)

**Sources:**
- https://www.cozycrimereads.com/cozy-mystery-101
- https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/masterpiece/specialfeatures/the-cozy-mystery-explained/
- https://rivereditor.com/guides/how-to-write-cozy-mystery-series-2026

## 14. MYSTERY/CRIME — Police Procedural (+ fair-play convention)

**Reader promise:** The investigation's mechanics are the spine — realistic(ish) police
process, team dynamics, institutional friction; the killer is caught and order restored.
The broader whodunit contract is **fair play**: the reader must get the same clues as the
detective (codified in Knox's Decalogue, 1929 — criminal introduced early, no reader-POV
culprit, no supernatural solutions, no undisclosed clues, the solution must be visible in
hindsight). (TV Tropes; Elizabeth Spann Craig; agathachristie.com)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000 (mystery/crime norm 70,000–90,000; procedurals run
the high side). (Manuscript Academy)

**Successful works:**
- *Cop Hater* (87th Precinct) — Ed McBain (1956). Canonical inventor of the ensemble procedural. [K]
- *The Black Echo* (Bosch) — Michael Connelly (1992). Standard-bearer: "procedural in architecture, noir in sensibility"; the reference modern series. (phillipstrang via search)

- *We Solve Murders* — Richard Osman (2024) / current wave of crime-with-warmth hybrids as the recent commercial direction. [K]

**Signature tropes:** the damaged-but-righteous detective; partner dynamics; interfering
brass/politics; the case that mirrors the detective's wound; autopsy/briefing-room beats;
the interrogation showdown. [K + MasterClass]

**Market signal:** Stable and huge — mystery/crime is a top-3 fiction category (one search
source values global mystery/thriller at $23B+ in 2024 — directional); tradpub-led with
strong library/audio; long series build careers. (Chapter blog via search)

## 15. MYSTERY/CRIME — Noir

**Reader promise:** Moral ambiguity as atmosphere: a compromised protagonist, systemic
rot, fatalism; justice is partial or pyrrhic — order is NOT restored (the anti-cozy).

**Word count (debut):** 60,000–90,000 — noir historically runs lean. [K]

## 16. THRILLER / SUSPENSE

**Reader promise:** Relentless pace, high stakes (life itself — eventually the
protagonist's), and time pressure: the "ticking clock" is a core convention (not always
literal, but the pressure of time must exist). Twists are mandatory; in psychological
thrillers the stakes shift to sanity/identity/loved ones, with unreliable narrators and
mind games. Stakes must be clear early. (savannahgilbo; MasterClass; Wikipedia)

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–100,000 (psychological/domestic can run 80–90k). (Manuscript Academy / Reedsy)

**Successful works:**
- *The Day of the Jackal* — Frederick Forsyth (1971) / *The Silence of the Lambs* — Thomas Harris (1988). Canonical procedural-thriller and serial-killer templates. [K]
- *Gone Girl* — Gillian Flynn (2012). Standard-bearer: 20M+ copies; created the domestic/psychological thriller boom and the unreliable-dual-POV template. (search evidence)
- *The Silent Patient* — Alex Michaelides (2019). Debut breakout proving the twist-driven psychological thriller's ongoing power. [K]
- *The Housemaid* — Freida McFadden (2022). Self-pub/KU phenomenon → paperback bestseller lists → 2025 film; the flagship indie thriller success. [K]

**Signature tropes:** ticking clock; the twist (mid + end); unreliable narrator; dual
timelines; domestic secrets ("dark secrets hidden in ordinary lives"); cat-and-mouse with
the villain; false ally. (savannahgilbo; infinitelibrary)

**Market signal:** Very large and stable (bundled mystery/thriller valued $23B+ globally
2024 per one source — directional); the 2010s domestic-thriller readership remains loyal;
strong in tradpub, supermarket paperback, KU (McFadden lane), and audio. (Chapter blog)

## 17. HORROR

**Reader promise:** Fear as the deliverable — dread, escalation, and bodily/existential
threat; the new psychological horror asks readers to question themselves and leaves
"existential dread that is nearly impossible to shake." Endings may be dark; survival is
not guaranteed (unlike thriller). Subgenre labels (quiet/cosmic/splatter) signal intensity
contracts. (Jane Friedman piece via search; [K])

**Word count (debut):** 70,000–100,000; the novella is a thriving commercial form
(Tor Nightfire/Kingfisher). [K]

**Successful works:**
- *The Shining* — Stephen King (1977). Canonical; King still accounts for ~12.7% of ALL US horror sales. (Bookseller via search)
- *Mexican Gothic* — Silvia Moreno-Garcia (2020). Breakout that signaled horror's diverse new mainstream. [K]
- *What Moves the Dead* / *What Feasts at Night* — T. Kingfisher (2022/2024). Instant NYT/USA Today bestsellers; proof of the novella lane. (search evidence)
- *Bury Your Gays* — Chuck Tingle (2024). USA Today bestseller, Locus Award Best Horror Novel — indie-origin author crossing to tradpub acclaim. (search evidence)

**Signature tropes:** the haunted house/bad place; possession; body horror; cosmic
indifference; final girl; small town with a secret; grief made literal. [K]

**Market signal:** Booming — biggest surge since the 1980s: US horror sales +24.2% (2023),
UK +54% (best year on record); slew of new tradpub horror imprints. Tradpub-led with

**Reader promise:** Character and theme over plot; prose itself as a source of pleasure

"contract" is depth and craft, validated socially by awards/critics rather than genre beats.

**Word count (debut):** 80,000–95,000 — agents' stated sweet spot for litfic debuts.
(Manuscript Academy via search)

**Successful works:**

## 21. WEB-SERIAL GENRES — LitRPG / Progression on Royal Road (platform economics)

**Reader promise (platform contract):** Consistent update cadence above all — "traction
depends on consistent output, not just a good hook"; long ongoing arcs (hundreds of
chapters); progression/LitRPG/isekai dominate; phone-readable chapters; visible power
growth per arc. Missing updates is the platform's HEA-break equivalent. (Jane Friedman;
RR forums)

**Format norms:** Chapters ~1,500–2,500 words; launch protocol ≈ 20,000 words dumped
fast, then a chapter/day for a month; backlog before launch. (RR forums / Medium guide)

**Monetization pipeline:** RR free serial → Patreon early access (3 tiers: $1–3 support /
$5 = 5–10 advance chapters / $10–15 = 15–20 chapters + extras; median 6 advance chapters;
~13% median follower→patron conversion; ≈$650/mo at 1,000 followers) → "stub" the serial
and publish compiled books to Kindle Unlimited → audiobook (Podium/Soundbooth; often the
largest revenue line) → tradpub print deals for the biggest hits (DCC→Ace/PRH, author kept
ebook rights). (Chapter Chronicles 750-story analysis; Jane Friedman; Wikipedia-DCC)

**Successful works:** *Mother of Learning* (foundational free serial); *Dungeon Crawler
Carl* (RR→10M+ copies, the genre's mainstream ambassador); *He Who Fights with Monsters*
(top KU earner); *Beware of Chicken* (cozy-progression hit); *Super Supportive* (current
RR original darling). [K + search evidence for DCC]

**Signature tropes:** system apocalypse; blue boxes/stat screens; dungeon dives; cultivation
sects; regression/time-loop do-overs; "numbers go up" catharsis; harem (declining) and
anti-harem tags; slice-of-life progression hybrids. [K + RR]

**Market signal:** Growing fast; the serial-first model is now a recognized author career
path (Jane Friedman coverage) and tradpub is buying print rights off RR charts. Sells:
Patreon + KU + audio; almost no tradpub-first activity. (Jane Friedman; Chapter Chronicles)

## CROSS-GENRE SYNTHESIS — the load-bearing genre-contract rules

1. **Romance HEA/HFN** — the hardest contract in fiction: central love story + emotionally
   satisfying optimistic ending, or it is not romance (RWA definition; reader backlash is
   categorical). Extends into romantasy: the couple must pay off by series end.
2. **Mystery fair play** — the reader gets every clue the detective gets (Knox's Decalogue);
   the solution must be visible in hindsight; and in non-noir crime, order is restored.
3. **The "cozy safety" contract (now cross-genre)** — no on-page gore/sex/profanity, no
   trauma inflicted on the reader, community + warm ending guaranteed; identical shape in
   cozy mystery and cozy fantasy — coziness is a *content-safety promise*, not a plot type.
4. **The progression/serial cadence contract** — visible, frequent power advancement plus
   reliable update rhythm; in serials, cadence breaches cost readership faster than craft
   flaws.
5. **The plausibility contracts** — hard SF (don't break known science), historical fiction
   (don't break the period), thriller (don't release the time pressure): each genre names
   the one axis on which the reader will not forgive cheating.
